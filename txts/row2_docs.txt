### 5. Final Summary

The European Commission’s 2025 policy, enacted under the International Procurement Instrument, bars Chinese IVD manufacturers from any public‑sector tender exceeding €5 million and limits Chinese‑origin inputs to 50 % of a product’s bill of materials. This creates a **significant regulatory barrier** for Mindray, Snibe, and other Chinese firms seeking large‑scale contracts, while still leaving smaller, decentralized purchases theoretically open. Public‑procurement budgets represent a multi‑trillion‑dollar opportunity that will now be largely inaccessible to Chinese suppliers, even as export data show that Chinese medical‑device shipments to the EU more than doubled from 2015‑2023, evidencing strong price‑driven **customer receptivity**. The policy is driven by a broader trade dispute—highlighted by the claim that 87 % of Chinese public contracts exclude EU products—so future market access may hinge on diplomatic reciprocity rather than commercial merit. Key gaps remain around the definition of “inputs,” the impact on reimbursement pathways, private‑sector demand, and perceived product quality. Strategically, Chinese IVD firms must pivot to **private‑sector channels, partnerships with EU distributors, or localisation of non‑Chinese components** to mitigate the risk of exclusion from high‑value public tenders while monitoring the geopolitical climate for possible policy shifts.
